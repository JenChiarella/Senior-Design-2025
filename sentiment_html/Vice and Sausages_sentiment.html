VICE AND SAUSAGES.

We should think ourselves culpable if we did not direct attention to the "awful disclosures" of the Police, contained in our last week's paper. Inspector VICE saw a cart, containing a dead body, or beast, he says, deliver its burden at a butcher's shop in this Town; the animal, of whatever kind, was dressed and hung in the back of the shop, with a cloth before it. Vice being suspicious, watched, and behold! in the outskirt of the town--at a back entrance to a cheap sausage shop, a cart again deposits its burden, containing, as he avers, part of the dead beast in question. Now, a veil of mystery hangs about this horrible tale, which the sausage-eating public have a right to demand should be withdrawn. In the first place, what was the beast? Was it an anti-temperance man? does any jovial circle mourn a boon companion? or what kind of beast was it? We have heard of a man who committed suicide by rashly converting himself into sausages in his own "patent-never-leaving-off sausage engine," and he, it is affirmed, did not spoil the sausages; but then he was a living man, and not a dead beast. Again, who was the butcher and who the vicious sausage maker, so well known to VICE, in the outskirts of the town? for we agree with the MAYOR, "that the odium of buying unwholesome meat ought not to be thrown unjustly upon the respectable shopkeepers in the Town." In the name, therefore, of every virtuous sausage maker--in the name of common justice--of England--of Great Britain at large, we say, Name! Name! Now, we know no reason why Inspector VICE should not do this; the Police themselves may be lovers of sausages, and ought to feel a pleasure in protecting the respectable manufacturers of that savoury article. The poet justly observes-- "Vice is a monster of so frightful mien, That to be hated needs but to be seen; But seen too oft, familiar with his face, We first endure, then pity, then embrace." Now, however familiar the cheap sausage shops may thus become with VICE, and to whatever embraces the Inspector may be subjected in consequence from the fair sausage markers, yet we trust that he will not become equally friendly with them, but at least let us know when "cats is in," that is, when they are considered to be in season in the outskirts of the Town; for there may be those among the lovers of this savoury meat who may not consider that the virtue lies all in the seasoning, and that the meat is nothing, and that whether the animal died in the natural way, by being killed, or from the neglect of sanitary regulations, is equally of no consequence. We agree with Mr. WELLER that every kind of animals, mice, rats, kittens, puppy dogs, up to a dead beast or a body, may all be made, by judicious seasoning, to taste like pork sausages; but there may be people with over-nice appetites, who object to them notwithstanding, and we think for their sake that the Police ought to give the fullest information as to where real pork (killed pigs) is used.
